Composite position: pattern vs feature-to-feature

What it controls

A two-line position FCF that simultaneously controls a pattern of features in two ways: where the whole pattern sits relative to the datums (a loose tolerance, the upper line) and where the features sit relative to each other (a tight tolerance, the lower line). One symbol, two zones, nested.

This is the right tool when, say, a four-hole bolt pattern needs to land roughly on the part (an outer zone of ±1 mm is fine for assembly) but the four holes need to be tightly aligned to each other so that the mating part's bolts can pass through (an inner zone of ±0.1 mm). Composite position expresses both budgets at once.

Look at the diagram

A composite FCF for a four-hole pattern. Upper line ⌖ Ø0.5 A B C defines a large blue cylindrical zone for each hole, oriented and located against the full datum reference frame. Lower line ⌖ Ø0.1 (no datums repeated explicitly under the rule — see lesson 36) defines a small inner zone that floats with the pattern: it controls feature-to-feature only.

Two snapshots: a pattern that's well-located and tightly grouped (passes both), and a pattern that's shifted off-pattern but still well-grouped (fails upper, passes lower — fails the FCF).

Common mistake

Reading both lines as if both zones were locked to the datums identically. They aren't. In Y14.5-2018, the lower line of a composite FCF is the feature-relating tolerance zone framework (FRTZF): it tightens feature-to-feature spacing inside the pattern. Repeated datums in that lower line govern orientation only, never location, so the pattern remains translationally free as a group. That's exactly what makes composite different from a multi-single-segment FCF (lesson 36). Read which one you've drawn carefully — the rule of thumb: one ⌖ symbol on the left, both rows; two separate ⌖ symbols stacked = multi-single-segment.

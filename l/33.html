Circular runout: one rotation, one slice

What it controls

Circular runout (⟳) is the total indicator reading (TIR) at a single cross-section as the part is rotated 360° about a datum axis. The tolerance value is the maximum allowed needle deflection (full-indicator-movement, FIM) at that slice. It catches a combination of circularity and coaxiality with the datum axis in one composite measurement — but only on a per-slice basis.

Practically: chuck the part on its datum, set an indicator against the surface, rotate one turn, watch the needle. If the swing is within the tolerance, that slice passes. Move the indicator to another slice and repeat.

Look at the diagram

A shaft mounted on datum axis A (V-blocks or chuck centerline). An indicator probe rests on the surface at one cross-section. As the shaft rotates, the surface variation (lobing + center offset from A) causes the needle to deflect. The total swing — high to low across the rotation — is the runout reading.

Below, a polar plot of the surface at one slice (relative to datum axis A) shows the wobble; the two concentric circles bounding it are 0.1 apart. The FCF reads ⟳ 0.1 A.

Common mistake

Believing circular runout controls the length of the shaft — it doesn't. Each slice is checked independently. A perfectly tapered cone could pass circular runout at every slice yet have terrible coaxiality along its length. That's what total runout (next lesson) is for. Reach for circular runout when only the local roundness-plus-coaxiality at a sealing or bearing-contact ring matters; reach for total runout when the whole surface has to run true.

The 3-2-1 principle: how three datums lock a part in space

What it controls

How a physical part — which floats freely in space with six degrees of freedom (three translations, three rotations) — gets fully constrained for inspection. The answer is 3-2-1: the primary datum contacts at three points, the secondary at two, the tertiary at one. Six contacts total. Six degrees of freedom removed.

A part that isn't fully constrained moves while you measure it. A datum reference frame that doesn't follow 3-2-1 either over-constrains (the part rocks between two equally valid positions) or under-constrains (a measurement depends on how the inspector happened to nudge it).

Look at the diagram

A rectangular block being seated against three orthogonal planes. The primary plane (datum A, bottom) takes three contact points — that's the minimum to define a plane, and it locks one translation (vertical) plus two rotations (pitch and roll). The secondary plane (datum B, back) takes two contacts along a line — one more translation gone, one more rotation gone. The tertiary plane (datum C, side) takes one contact at a point — the final translation locked. Total: 3 + 2 + 1 = 6 contacts, 6 degrees of freedom removed.

Notice the order matters: the primary gets the most contact because it's most trusted to be flat and to represent function.

Common mistake

Treating all three datums as equivalent. They aren't — they're in priority order. The primary fully seats first; the secondary aligns against the primary; the tertiary just stops the slide. If you swap primary and tertiary, you get a different inspection result because the part now rocks on what should have been the stable reference.

Cylindricity: roundness plus straightness plus taper

What it controls

How truly cylindrical a surface of revolution is. The tolerance zone is two concentric cylinders a radial distance t apart, and the entire surface of the feature must lie between them. Cylindricity catches everything circularity misses — taper, banana, barrel, hourglass — and rolls roundness, straightness of every line element, and lengthwise constancy of size into one inspection.

No datum, like the other form controls. The two cylinders float in space to find the tightest enclosure.

Look at the diagram

A side view of a real shaft, slightly tapered and slightly bowed (exaggerated for clarity). Two concentric cylinders (blue) sleeve the feature — the outer one touches the highest points, the inner one touches the lowest. The radial gap t is the cylindricity tolerance. Notice this is one pair of cylinders for the whole length, not a stack of independent circularity slices.

To the right, a small ledger compares: circularity = round per slice; straightness (axis) = axis in a tube; cylindricity = both, plus no taper, plus no waist.

Common mistake

Specifying cylindricity when circularity is what you actually wanted. Cylindricity is much harder (and more expensive) to inspect because it composes three independent errors into one zone. If only the roundness of each cross-section matters — for example, a seal lip that only contacts one ring — use circularity. Reserve cylindricity for surfaces that mate or seal over their full length.

Diameter symbol: round zone vs square zone

What it controls

The shape of the position tolerance zone. With Ø in the tolerance block, the zone is a cylinder — round in cross-section. Without Ø, the zone is two parallel planes — effectively a thin slab, useful for slots and non-cylindrical features. For a hole or pin, you almost always want Ø.

This one symbol is the most consequential character on most FCFs. Adding it turns a coordinate-style 0.2×0.2 square into a Ø0.2 circle: same limit on the axes, but tighter in the corners because the circular zone is inscribed in the square.

Look at the diagram

Two zones drawn at the same scale. Left: the implied square zone from plus/minus tolerancing — ±0.1 on X and ±0.1 on Y — a 0.2 × 0.2 square. Right: a Ø0.2 round zone from position-with-diameter. The circle touches the square on the axes but cuts off the square's diagonal corners.

Two example hole-center dots illustrate the consequence: a center at (0.09, 0.09) is inside the square (each axis is ≤ 0.1) but outside the circle (radius ≈ 0.127, > 0.1). Same measured X/Y coordinates. The +/- scheme accepts it; the position-Ø scheme rejects it because the radial error is too large.

Common mistake

Omitting Ø when you wanted a round zone. Without Ø, the position tolerance is treated as two parallel planes — a slab, not a cylinder. The inspector dutifully checks against the wrong zone shape, and parts that should have passed get rejected, while parts that shouldn't have passed slip through (depending on the orientation of the slab). On any axis-symmetric feature, the Ø is mandatory.

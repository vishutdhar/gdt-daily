Profile of a line: 2D control on a curved feature

What it controls

Profile of a line (⌒) controls the shape of a single cross-section of a feature relative to a basic profile — the ideal curve defined by basic dimensions on the drawing. The tolerance zone is two offset curves, parallel to the basic profile, with the tolerance value (e.g. 0.2) as the total width between them. The actual surface, sliced at any cross-section, must lie inside that 2D band.

This is the airfoil/molding/cam control. Each slice of the part has to follow the curve, but slices don't have to be related to each other — that's what profile of a surface (next lesson) is for.

Look at the diagram

A curved aerodynamic profile (basic curve, cream) with two offset curves (blue dashed) drawn ±t/2 on either side of it. A few cross-section slices show the actual surface (cream solid) wandering inside the zone — at each slice, the surface stays between the two offsets.

The FCF reads ⌒ 0.2 A. No Ø needed (profile of a line is inherently a 2D zone). Datum A orients the basic profile in space.

Common mistake

Confusing profile of a line with profile of a surface. Profile of a line is checked at each cross-section independently — one slice can lean one way and the next slice the other, as long as each slice stays in its own 2D zone. Profile of a surface ties them together — the whole 3D surface must fit between two offset surfaces. If you want to control twist or warpage along the length of a curved feature, you almost always want surface, not line.

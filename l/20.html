Circularity (roundness): two concentric circles per slice

What it controls

How round a surface of revolution is — at a single cross-section. Take a cylinder, sphere, or cone; slice it perpendicular to its axis; the resulting profile must lie between two concentric circles that are radially t apart. Repeat for every cross-section. Circularity is a per-slice control — nothing in the symbol says the slices have to share the same center, only that each one passes on its own.

No datum. Like flatness, the two circles float to find the tightest fit for each slice.

Look at the diagram

A single cross-section of a cylinder shown end-on. The real profile is wavy (exaggerated — three-lobed, like a Reuleaux triangle's evil twin). Two concentric circles (blue) enclose the profile: an outer one touching the highest points, an inner one touching the lowest. The radial gap between them is t. The center of the two circles is the mean center of that slice, not a datum.

To the side, a side view of the same cylinder shows three slicing planes — three independent circularity checks, each with its own pair of circles.

Common mistake

Treating circularity as if it shares a center with the part's axis. It doesn't — each slice gets its own pair of concentric circles, found independently. A part can pass circularity at every cross-section and still be banana-shaped (that's what cylindricity catches). And a part can be a perfect cylinder in cross-section but tapered along its length and still pass circularity. The control is 2D, even though the part is 3D.

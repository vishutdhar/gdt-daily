Profile of a surface: the swiss-army knife

What it controls

Profile of a surface (⌓) is the most versatile control in GD&T. The tolerance zone is two offset surfaces following the basic profile, with the actual surface required to lie entirely inside. With datums referenced, a single ⌓ FCF can simultaneously control form, size, orientation, and location — replacing what would otherwise take three or four separate callouts.

This is the symbol that shows up on castings, sheet-metal trim edges, lens surfaces, and any time a feature has a non-prismatic shape that needs to mate, seat, or seal.

Look at the diagram

A curved 3D surface (cream) shown with its basic profile, wrapped by two parallel offset surfaces (blue) that form the 3D tolerance zone. The actual surface sits inside, no matter where you measure it.

Side-by-side: a comparison of what one ⌓ A B FCF covers versus the alternative — flatness + perpendicularity + position + size. One symbol does the work of four. The catch: with datums, profile is no longer just a form control; it locks the feature in space.

Common mistake

Treating profile of a surface as if it only controls shape. Once you reference a datum, the zone is anchored in space relative to that datum — the feature must not just have the right curve, it must be in the right place and the right orientation. Engineers sometimes apply ⌓ A B C expecting only form control and are surprised when a part fails inspection for being shifted 0.3mm. If you only want form, leave the datums off (like flatness).

Tolerance zone: diameter vs surface

What it controls

The shape of the tolerance zone, not its size. With a diameter symbol (Ø) in the tolerance block, the zone is a cylinder that contains the feature's axis. Without Ø, the zone is two parallel planes — a slab — that contain the surface or center plane. Same numeric value, very different geometry.

That little Ø is one of the highest-leverage characters on a GD&T drawing. It rewrites what the tolerance means.

Look at the diagram

Two FCFs sit side by side, both reading "0.25 | A | B | C", and both controlling the position of a hole's axis. The left frame has Ø0.25 in the tolerance block — the zone drawn below is a cylinder of diameter 0.25, and the axis is in spec as long as it lives anywhere inside that cylinder. The right frame has just 0.25, no Ø — the zone is a slab between two parallel planes 0.25 apart, and the axis must fall inside that slab.

Notice the slab has direction; the cylinder does not. The slab implicitly tells you which plane the tolerance is measured against. The cylinder is rotationally symmetric — perfect for round features.

Common mistake

Reading "0.25" on a hole's position and assuming it's cylindrical because the hole itself is round. The hole being round does not make the zone round. The zone shape comes from the tolerance block, not the feature. If you don't see Ø, the zone is a slab — and a slab is far more restrictive in one direction and far more permissive in the other than a cylinder of the same nominal value.

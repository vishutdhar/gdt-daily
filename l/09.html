Primary, secondary, tertiary: order changes the answer

What it controls

The sequence in which datums are referenced inside a feature control frame. In the FCF | ⌖ | Ø0.2 Ⓜ | A | B | C |, the order A B C is not decoration — it tells the inspector exactly how the part is to be seated, and it tells the designer which surface dominates which.

The same part, measured against A B C and against B A C, can produce different numbers and a different pass/fail. Order is a measurement decision, not a labeling one.

Look at the diagram

The same L-shaped bracket inspected two ways. On the left, A primary, B secondary: the bracket seats flat on its broad bottom face (A), then slides until the back face (B) touches the secondary plane. The hole is measured perpendicular to A. On the right, B primary, A secondary: now the back face is what seats first, and the bottom face only stops the rotation. The hole's position relative to the datum reference frame is different — even though the part itself never changed.

When the primary changes, the whole frame rotates. Every downstream measurement rotates with it.

Common mistake

Picking datum order by surface size or labeling alphabetically. The primary should be the surface that most controls function in the assembly — usually the largest mating face that resists the most force, or the face that lands first during installation. Secondary stops a remaining rotation. Tertiary stops the final slide. Function dictates order; the alphabet just labels it.

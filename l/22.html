Perpendicularity: 90° to a datum, within a zone

What it controls

How square a feature is to a datum. Perpendicularity constrains a surface, line, or axis to lie within a tolerance zone that is exactly 90° to a datum plane or axis. The zone shape depends on what's being controlled:

- A planar surface → two parallel planes, t apart, perpendicular to the datum. - An axis or center plane → two parallel planes for a center plane, or a cylinder of diameter t for an axis.

Unlike flatness, perpendicularity always requires a datum — there's no "perpendicular to what?" without one. The 90° is basic (exact, implied), not a +/- value.

Look at the diagram

A block with a tall vertical wall sitting on a horizontal datum (datum A, the seating surface). The wall must be perpendicular to A. The tolerance zone is two parallel planes, 0.05 apart, both rigorously square to A. The real wall (cream) tilts slightly off vertical, but it stays inside the blue zone.

Below: the same geometry but the FCF is on a Ø hole going through the block. Now the controlled feature is an axis, and the zone is a cylinder Ø0.05 standing perpendicular to A. The hole's axis can wobble inside that cylinder.

Common mistake

Forgetting that perpendicularity controls only the orientation, not the location. A wall can be perfectly perpendicular to A and still be in the wrong place along the part — perpendicularity won't catch that. Combine it with position (or use profile of a surface) when location matters too. Use perpendicularity by itself when you only care that the feature stands square.

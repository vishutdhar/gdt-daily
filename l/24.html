Angularity: any basic angle, exactly

What it controls

Orientation at any other angle — 30°, 45°, 60°, 12.5° — relative to a datum. Angularity is the general orientation control; perpendicularity (90°) and parallelism (0°) are special cases of it. The tolerance zone is identical in shape to those two — two parallel planes for a surface, a cylinder for an axis — but tilted to the basic angle stated on the drawing.

A "basic" angle is one shown inside a rectangle on the drawing, like [45°]. It carries no plus/minus of its own; the angularity FCF is what gives it a tolerance.

Look at the diagram

A part with a sloped face at a basic angle of [30°] to datum A (the horizontal seating face). The angularity tolerance zone is two parallel planes, both tilted at exactly 30° to A, with t = 0.1 between them. The real sloped face sits inside the zone, free to wave a little but always cradled by the two ideal planes at the exact angle.

Below the part: a callout showing the difference between an angle in a rectangle ([30°], basic, no tolerance) and an angle with ± (toleranced by ±, a different and more permissive scheme).

Common mistake

Putting +/− on the angle dimension and an angularity FCF on the feature. They fight: the ± lets the angle vary; the angularity locks it. Only one tolerancing scheme per feature. If you use angularity, draw the angle as a basic dimension; if you use ±, drop the angularity. Modern Y14.5 practice strongly prefers basic + angularity for any feature that has a function tied to a precise angle.

Unilateral vs bilateral profile: which side of the line

What it controls

The default profile zone is equally bilateral — half the tolerance sits inside the basic profile, half outside. Often the function requires something different: maybe the part can only grow outward (a casting that gets machined), or only inward (a hole that must stay big enough). The Y14.5-2018 way to say so is with phantom lines drawn beside the basic profile, plus the Ⓤ unequally-disposed profile modifier when the zone is not equal about the true profile.

Three configurations cover most drawings: equal bilateral (default), unilateral outside (zone sits entirely outside the basic profile), unilateral inside (zone entirely inside). All use the same FCF symbol — only the phantom-line indication changes.

Look at the diagram

Three side-by-side curved features, identical FCF (⌓ 0.4 A B), differing only in zone placement. Bilateral: zone straddles the basic profile, ±0.2 each side. Unilateral outside: zone is offset 0.4 on the "more material" side. Unilateral inside: zone is offset 0.4 on the "less material" side. The phantom lines (thin chain-dot) on the drawing show which side the zone occupies.

A small inset shows the Y14.5-2018 unequally-disposed notation: ⌓ 0.4 Ⓤ 0.1 A B means a total profile zone of 0.4, with 0.1 in the plus direction and the remaining 0.3 on the opposite side.

Common mistake

Forgetting to indicate the zone offset and defaulting to bilateral when function actually wants unilateral. A casting that gets machined later needs all stock on the outside — drawn without phantom lines, the inspector reads bilateral and a casting at the inner extreme has less stock than the machinist needs. The fix is one phantom line away. When in doubt, draw the phantom lines; the default is rarely wrong but never wastes ink to confirm.

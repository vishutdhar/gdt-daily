Concentricity and symmetry: deprecated, use position

What it controls

Two symbols you'll see on older drawings but should not specify on new ones. In Y14.5-2018, both concentricity (◎) and symmetry (≑) were officially removed — the standard now recommends position or runout for everything they used to control. The reason: both legacy symbols controlled the derived median points of opposed surface elements, which is nearly impossible to measure repeatably on a real part.

The replacements are simpler and more inspectable. For "this axis is on that axis" → position with the appropriate Ø zone. For "the runout of this surface relative to that axis is bounded" → circular or total runout. For "this center plane is on that center plane" → position on the slot's center plane.

Look at the diagram

Three side-by-side legacy callouts crossed out, each paired with its modern replacement. Concentricity (◎ Ø0.1 A) → position (⌖ Ø0.1 A) when location matters, or runout when surface wobble matters. Symmetry (≑ 0.1 A) → position (⌖ 0.1 A) on the center plane. Same intent, simpler math, repeatable on a CMM.

A small note flags that these symbols still appear on legacy drawings: read them, interpret them, but don't draw them.

Common mistake

Carrying a concentricity callout forward when redrawing or updating a part. The replacement isn't always obvious — concentricity is not the same as position even when both have Ø zones, because position controls the axis derived from the surface, while concentricity controlled median points opposing each other. Position is what you want 99% of the time. Reserve runout for cases where surface variation in rotation is the actual concern.

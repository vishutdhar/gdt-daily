Parallelism: same shape, parallel to a datum

What it controls

How parallel a feature is to a datum. Parallelism uses the same tolerance-zone shapes as perpendicularity — two parallel planes for a surface or center plane, a cylinder for an axis — but the zone is oriented parallel to the datum instead of square to it. The angle implied is 0° (or 180°), basic, no tolerance on the angle itself.

It's the most-used orientation control for stacked-up assemblies, slide surfaces, and any pair of features that need to stay coplanar or coaxial under load.

Look at the diagram

A wedge-shaped part sitting on datum A (the bottom). The top surface is slightly tipped — exaggerated. The parallelism FCF says the top must be parallel to A within 0.05. The tolerance zone is two planes, 0.05 apart, both parallel to A. The real top surface sits between them.

To the right, the cylindrical version: a hole whose axis must be parallel to datum B (an edge or another axis). Zone is a Ø0.05 cylinder parallel to B. The hole's axis is allowed to swim inside that cylinder so long as it doesn't tip.

Common mistake

Using parallelism when you meant flatness. Parallelism requires a datum; flatness doesn't. If the surface is a sealing face and the only thing you care about is that it's not warped, flatness is sufficient and cheaper. Parallelism specifically guards the relationship to another feature. Pick parallelism when the relationship matters, flatness when only the surface itself does.

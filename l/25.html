Position: the workhorse of GD&T

What it controls

Where a feature's axis, center plane, or center point sits relative to a datum reference frame. Position is the most-used GD&T symbol in industry — virtually every hole, slot, pin, or boss carries one, and it replaces the old plus/minus-on-X-and-Y scheme used in pre-GD&T drawings.

The basic dimensions (boxed) on the drawing say where the feature is supposed to be. The position FCF then names the tolerance zone the actual feature can fall inside.

Look at the diagram

A plate with a hole. The drawing locates the hole at basic dimensions [30] from datum B (left edge) and [20] from datum C (bottom edge), measured against datum A (the seating face). The position FCF reads | ⌖ | Ø0.2 | A | B | C |.

Centered on the basic location, a small blue cylinder (shown end-on as a circle) is the tolerance zone — Ø0.2 wide, perpendicular to A. The hole's true axis must lie inside that cylinder. The hole itself can be anywhere as long as its axis is inside the zone.

Common mistake

Reading the position value as a tolerance on the X and Y coordinates separately. It is not — Ø0.2 is the diameter of a round zone. Compared to ±0.1 on X and ±0.1 on Y (a 0.2×0.2 square), the Ø0.2 round zone has the same maximum offset along the axes but is tighter at the diagonals — about 21% less area than the square. If you want the round zone that just covers the old coordinate square, the diameter is about Ø0.283, not Ø0.2.

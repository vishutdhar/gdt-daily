The limit problem: why +/- isn't enough

What it controls

Plus/minus tolerancing on x and y produces a square tolerance zone. Functional fit (a bolt going through a hole, a pin landing in a bushing) needs a round one. GD&T's job is to describe the zone shape, not just the dimensions.

Look at the diagram

The same hole, two languages. Same nominal position, same ±0.10 axis reach — but the round position zone has 57% more area than the square. The asymmetry isn't at the corners (the round zone's radius is 0.141 — it touches the square's corners exactly); it's along the axes. A part 0.13 off in x with y dead-on fails the ±0.10 square but passes Ø0.283 round. Functional fit only cares about radial offset from nominal, not x and y independently — so the square wrongly rejects parts the bolt would actually accept.

Common mistake

"Plus/minus is tighter, so it must be better." Wrong direction. ±0.10 / ±0.10 isn't tighter — it's the wrong shape: looser at the diagonals (0.141) than along the axes (0.10), splitting one radial constraint into two unrelated ones. GD&T position replaces the square with the right-shape zone — one radial number that matches how the bolt actually sees the hole.

Lesson 02 · §01 · Why GD&T exists Published · 2026-05-15 ↗ permalink

Two languages on one drawing

What it controls

GD&T does not replace +/-. Every drawing has both: size tolerances (how big) and geometric tolerances (form, orientation, location). They work together.

Look at the diagram

The diameter of the hole still uses ±. The position of the hole uses GD&T. Size and location are separate concerns; the drawing says both at once.

Common mistake

Reading the FCF and ignoring the size tolerance — or reading the size tolerance and ignoring the FCF. A part can be in-tolerance on size but out-of-tolerance on position, and vice versa.

Anatomy of the Feature Control Frame

What it controls

Every geometric tolerance lives in a Feature Control Frame (FCF) — a row of boxes that reads like a sentence: "This [symbol] of this feature, by this amount [modifier], relative to these datums."

Look at the diagram

Five compartments, left to right: symbol → tolerance (with optional modifier) → primary datum → secondary datum → tertiary datum.

Common mistake

Reading datums in alphabetical order instead of FCF order. The leftmost datum is the primary regardless of the letter — |A|B|C| and |C|B|A| mean different things on the same part.

Tolerance zone: diameter vs surface

What it controls

The shape of the tolerance zone, not its size. With a diameter symbol (Ø) in the tolerance block, the zone is a cylinder that contains the feature's axis. Without Ø, the zone is two parallel planes — a slab — that contain the surface or center plane. Same numeric value, very different geometry.

That little Ø is one of the highest-leverage characters on a GD&T drawing. It rewrites what the tolerance means.

Look at the diagram

Two FCFs sit side by side, both reading "0.25 | A | B | C", and both controlling the position of a hole's axis. The left frame has Ø0.25 in the tolerance block — the zone drawn below is a cylinder of diameter 0.25, and the axis is in spec as long as it lives anywhere inside that cylinder. The right frame has just 0.25, no Ø — the zone is a slab between two parallel planes 0.25 apart, and the axis must fall inside that slab.

Notice the slab has direction; the cylinder does not. The slab implicitly tells you which plane the tolerance is measured against. The cylinder is rotationally symmetric — perfect for round features.

Common mistake

Reading "0.25" on a hole's position and assuming it's cylindrical because the hole itself is round. The hole being round does not make the zone round. The zone shape comes from the tolerance block, not the feature. If you don't see Ø, the zone is a slab — and a slab is far more restrictive in one direction and far more permissive in the other than a cylinder of the same nominal value.

Datum vs datum feature

What it controls

A vocabulary distinction that fixes 80% of beginner confusion: the datum feature and the datum are not the same thing.

The datum feature is the physical surface on your part — the actual machined face, hole, or cylinder, with all its real-world imperfections. The datum is the theoretically perfect geometric reference (a plane, axis, or point) that you derive from contact with that surface during inspection. The drawing labels the datum feature; the inspection establishes the datum.

Look at the diagram

The left side shows a real part — a wavy, imperfect bottom surface labeled "datum feature A" with a small datum-feature symbol (the framed letter A) attached to it. The right side shows what happens during inspection: the part is set on a surface plate, and the highest three contact points of that wavy surface define the perfect theoretical plane — that plane is datum A.

The plane is perfect; the surface is not. The plane is what the FCF "A | B | C" actually refers to.

Common mistake

Treating the wavy real surface as if it were the plane in your tolerance calculation. The inspector doesn't measure to the surface — they measure to the simulator (the surface plate) that establishes the datum. If you have any feel for what "tangent to a surface" means: the datum is tangent, the feature is not.

Lesson 06 · §03 · Datums Published · 2026-05-19 ↗ permalink

Picking datum features: function over convenience

What it controls

Which surfaces of your part should carry datum-feature labels. The rule: pick the surfaces that match how the part is used, not the ones that are easy to measure. A datum feature should represent the assembly's mating surface, the seating face on the next part up, the bore that the shaft pilots through.

When datums match function, inspection mirrors assembly. When datums are picked for convenience, you can have a part that measures "in spec" on a CMM and still fails to fit.

Look at the diagram

Two versions of the same bracket. The left version labels the mating face (the surface that lands on the next part in the assembly) and the pilot hole (which centers the bracket on a shoulder bolt) as datums A and B. The right version labels the broadest top face and an outside edge as A and B because they were the easiest to set up on the inspection table.

Both parts can pass inspection. Only the left one is guaranteed to fit.

Common mistake

Defaulting to the largest, flattest surface as datum A because "more contact = more stability." Sometimes true, often not — if that surface never touches anything in service, you've just picked a reference that doesn't predict fit. Always ask: what does this surface touch in the next assembly up?

Lesson 07 · §03 · Datums Published · 2026-05-20 ↗ permalink

Datum simulators: how datums get real

What it controls

The bridge between the drawing and the inspection lab. A datum is theoretical — a perfect plane, a perfect axis, a perfect point. You cannot machine a perfect anything. So during inspection, the datum is simulated by a piece of high-precision hardware: a surface plate, a gage pin, a V-block, a chuck.

During inspection, the simulator is the datum. The simulator's geometry — flat to a few millionths, round to a tenth — stands in for the theoretical reference, and the part's measurements are taken relative to the simulator.

Look at the diagram

Three rows, one per datum type. Top: a planar datum feature sits on a surface plate — the plate's perfect top surface establishes datum A. Middle: a cylindrical datum feature (a shaft) is held in a three-jaw chuck that contracts symmetrically; the chuck's centerline establishes the datum axis. Bottom: a hole datum feature receives a gage pin with a slip-fit; the pin's axis establishes the datum axis.

Different geometry, same idea: a precise simulator replaces a perfect abstraction.

Common mistake

Treating the surface plate or gage pin as just "a tool." It is the datum during that inspection. If your surface plate is worn or your gage pin is undersized, your datum is wrong — and every measurement traced to it inherits that error. Calibration of simulators is calibration of datums.

The 3-2-1 principle: how three datums lock a part in space

What it controls

How a physical part — which floats freely in space with six degrees of freedom (three translations, three rotations) — gets fully constrained for inspection. The answer is 3-2-1: the primary datum contacts at three points, the secondary at two, the tertiary at one. Six contacts total. Six degrees of freedom removed.

A part that isn't fully constrained moves while you measure it. A datum reference frame that doesn't follow 3-2-1 either over-constrains (the part rocks between two equally valid positions) or under-constrains (a measurement depends on how the inspector happened to nudge it).

Look at the diagram

A rectangular block being seated against three orthogonal planes. The primary plane (datum A, bottom) takes three contact points — that's the minimum to define a plane, and it locks one translation (vertical) plus two rotations (pitch and roll). The secondary plane (datum B, back) takes two contacts along a line — one more translation gone, one more rotation gone. The tertiary plane (datum C, side) takes one contact at a point — the final translation locked. Total: 3 + 2 + 1 = 6 contacts, 6 degrees of freedom removed.

Notice the order matters: the primary gets the most contact because it's most trusted to be flat and to represent function.

Common mistake

Treating all three datums as equivalent. They aren't — they're in priority order. The primary fully seats first; the secondary aligns against the primary; the tertiary just stops the slide. If you swap primary and tertiary, you get a different inspection result because the part now rocks on what should have been the stable reference.

Lesson 09 · §04 · Datum Reference Frame Published · 2026-05-22 ↗ permalink

Primary, secondary, tertiary: order changes the answer

What it controls

The sequence in which datums are referenced inside a feature control frame. In the FCF | ⌖ | Ø0.2 Ⓜ | A | B | C |, the order A B C is not decoration — it tells the inspector exactly how the part is to be seated, and it tells the designer which surface dominates which.

The same part, measured against A B C and against B A C, can produce different numbers and a different pass/fail. Order is a measurement decision, not a labeling one.

Look at the diagram

The same L-shaped bracket inspected two ways. On the left, A primary, B secondary: the bracket seats flat on its broad bottom face (A), then slides until the back face (B) touches the secondary plane. The hole is measured perpendicular to A. On the right, B primary, A secondary: now the back face is what seats first, and the bottom face only stops the rotation. The hole's position relative to the datum reference frame is different — even though the part itself never changed.

When the primary changes, the whole frame rotates. Every downstream measurement rotates with it.

Common mistake

Picking datum order by surface size or labeling alphabetically. The primary should be the surface that most controls function in the assembly — usually the largest mating face that resists the most force, or the face that lands first during installation. Secondary stops a remaining rotation. Tertiary stops the final slide. Function dictates order; the alphabet just labels it.

Material condition: tolerance that grows with the part

What it controls

Whether the size of a feature can buy you extra geometric tolerance. Most GD&T callouts come with a stated tolerance — say, position within Ø0.2. A material condition modifier (Ⓜ for MMC, Ⓛ for LMC) flips a switch that says: if the feature is made looser than its tightest material limit, the part earns additional tolerance equal to that departure.

In plain terms: a hole drilled a little oversize is easier to align with whatever goes through it, so it's allowed to be a little more off-center. The drawing acknowledges the physics of assembly instead of pretending every measurement is independent.

Look at the diagram

A horizontal bar shows feature size sweeping from MMC (most material) on the left to LMC (least material) on the right. The stated tolerance is a fixed band (cream). Underneath, the allowed tolerance under Ⓜ is a wedge that widens as size moves toward LMC — every micrometer the hole grows past its MMC size adds the same amount to the position tolerance. That extra width is called bonus tolerance (blue).

A third row shows RFS (no modifier): the band stays the same width regardless of size. Same drawing, very different floor.

Common mistake

Reading Ⓜ as "looser tolerance everywhere." It is not. At MMC size, the allowed tolerance is exactly the stated value — no bonus. The bonus only appears as the feature departs from MMC. Parts produced right at the tight end of the size range still have to hit position within Ø0.2 cold. The gift only arrives when there's slack to claim.

Lesson 11 · §05 · Material Condition: MMC Published · 2026-05-24 ↗ permalink

MMC defined: hole versus pin

What it controls

The exact meaning of Maximum Material Condition. The phrase trips up everyone the first time: "maximum material" doesn't mean biggest dimension, it means most material left on the part. For an external feature (a pin, a boss, a shaft), more material means a larger size. For an internal feature (a hole, a slot, a bore), more material means a smaller size — the metal hasn't been removed yet.

Get this backwards and every bonus-tolerance calculation, every virtual-condition number, every functional gage you design is off in the wrong direction.

Look at the diagram

Two columns. On the left, a hole sweeps from its smallest size (most material remaining around it) to its largest. MMC is at the left end. On the right, a pin sweeps from its smallest size to its largest. MMC is at the right end. The arrows point in opposite directions but the rule is the same: pick the size at which the part weighs more.

A simple rule lives below: MMC = the size of the feature when assembly would be hardest. A small hole and a large pin both make assembly harder — that's MMC.

Common mistake

Memorizing "MMC = max size" because it works for pins, then applying it to holes. It will work half the time. The correct mental model is mass, not dimension. If you can't remember which way the arrow points on a given feature, ask: which size makes this harder to fit with its mate? That's MMC, every time.

Lesson 12 · §05 · Material Condition: MMC Published · 2026-05-24 ↗ permalink

Bonus tolerance: the gift for staying loose

What it controls

The arithmetic of bonus tolerance. When a feature controlled at MMC is produced larger than its MMC size (for a hole) or smaller than its MMC size (for a pin), the difference is added 1:1 to the geometric tolerance. The drawing said Ø0.2; on a real part you might be allowed Ø0.3, Ø0.5, even Ø0.6, depending on how much the feature drifted toward LMC.

This is the most concrete economic argument for GD&T over plus/minus. With Ⓜ in the FCF, parts that would be scrapped as "out of position" under a tight box tolerance can be functional and in-spec — because they were sized in a way that makes them easier to assemble.

Look at the diagram

A worked example for a hole specified as Ø8.0–8.4 with a position tolerance of Ø0.2 at MMC. The table walks four manufactured sizes from MMC up to LMC. At every step, the bonus equals the departure from MMC, and the total allowed position tolerance equals stated + bonus. At Ø8.4 (LMC), the hole earns Ø0.4 of bonus on top of the Ø0.2 stated — a total of Ø0.6 of position tolerance, three times the original.

Underneath, two circles visualize the tolerance zone at MMC and at LMC — same FCF, same drawing, same part family, very different zones.

Common mistake

Stacking bonus on top of a stated tolerance that doesn't have Ⓜ. No modifier in the tolerance block means RFS by default in ASME Y14.5-2018 — and RFS gets zero bonus, ever. The bonus mechanic is unlocked only by writing Ⓜ (or Ⓛ) after the tolerance value. No symbol, no gift.

Virtual condition: the worst-case mating size

What it controls

The single number that answers the question "will it actually fit?" Virtual condition is the worst-case envelope of a feature, combining its material-condition size with its geometric tolerance: for an external feature, VC = MMC + position tolerance; for an internal feature, VC = MMC − position tolerance.

This number is what a functional gage is built to. A pin gage cut to the hole's virtual condition either drops into every in-spec part or doesn't — no measurement, no math, no negotiation. The gage encodes the worst case the drawing allows, and the part either passes the boundary or it doesn't.

Look at the diagram

A hole specified at Ø8.0 MMC with a position tolerance of Ø0.2 Ⓜ. The virtual condition is Ø7.8 — the smallest hole that could exist while still meeting the spec, made up of the smallest allowed hole displaced by the largest allowed position error. A pin gage at Ø7.8 must pass through every conforming hole, regardless of how that hole's size and position errors trade against each other.

The right side shows the mating pin's side of the same story: a pin at Ø7.8 MMC with Ø0.2 Ⓜ has a virtual condition of Ø8.0 — exactly the hole's MMC. Pin worst-case (Ø8.0) equals the hole at MMC (Ø8.0) — line-to-line at the MMC boundary. The full floating-fastener guarantee (Y14.5-2018 App. B) is pin VC ≤ hole VC: for this hole, a guaranteed pin would need VC ≤ Ø7.8.

Common mistake

Confusing virtual condition with MMC or with the stated tolerance alone. MMC is just the size; the stated tolerance is just the position. Virtual condition is the boundary — the single number that says whether the mate works under the worst legal combination of size and position. If you're building gages or doing fit calculations, this is the number you need.

LMC defined: the mirror image of MMC

What it controls

The symmetric partner to MMC. Least Material Condition is the size at which a feature has the least material on it: for a hole, that's the largest allowed diameter (more metal has been removed); for a pin, that's the smallest allowed diameter (less metal is left). The Ⓛ modifier in an FCF behaves like Ⓜ in reverse — bonus tolerance is earned as the feature departs from LMC back toward MMC.

LMC isn't about assembly clearance; it's about keeping enough material for a downstream concern: wall thickness, thread engagement, electrical clearance, strength.

Look at the diagram

Two columns mirroring the MMC diagram from lesson 11. For the hole, sweep size from smallest to largest — LMC is at the right (largest hole, thinnest surrounding wall). For the pin, sweep size from smallest to largest — LMC is at the left (smallest pin, least metal remaining).

The rule strip at the bottom: LMC = the size at which the part has the least material to give. That's the size that worries you when wall thickness, edge distance, or minimum stock is the design constraint.

Common mistake

Treating LMC and MMC as interchangeable choices for "the bonus modifier." They aren't — they point at different worst cases. Use MMC when assembly is the worry (the loosest mate is the hardest). Use LMC when material is the worry (the thinnest section breaks). Pick the wrong one and your bonus tolerance flows toward the wrong end of the size range.

When LMC matters: wall thickness and edge distance

What it controls

The design situations where Ⓛ is the right answer instead of Ⓜ. Three classics: wall thickness between a hole and an edge or another hole, thread engagement on a tapped hole that mustn't grow too large, and minimum stock for downstream operations like coining or threading.

In all three, the worry isn't "will it fit?" — it's "will there be enough metal left?" That's exactly the question LMC was built to answer.

Look at the diagram

A bracket with a hole positioned near a finished edge. Two scenarios sit side by side. Left, position at Ⓜ: the hole is allowed to drift toward the edge as it grows, because the bonus is calculated against MMC. At LMC size, the wall is dangerously thin. Right, position at Ⓛ: the bonus only kicks in as the hole shrinks back toward MMC, so the worst case — biggest hole at LMC — gets no bonus position tolerance. Wall thickness is protected.

The shaded "danger strip" along the edge shows the minimum wall the designer needs. Under Ⓜ, the strip is breached at worst case. Under Ⓛ, it isn't.

Common mistake

Reaching for MMC because "MMC is the standard one." Standard for assembly, yes. But if the function the drawing has to protect is material remaining, MMC actively rewards the failure mode — it hands out bonus tolerance precisely as the wall gets thinner. Read the part's job before you pick the modifier. Wall thickness, edge distance, thread engagement → LMC.

Bonus under LMC: identical math, opposite direction

What it controls

The bonus-tolerance calculation when Ⓛ is in the FCF. Every micrometer the feature departs from LMC back toward MMC is added 1:1 to the stated geometric tolerance. The arithmetic is identical to MMC bonus; only the reference point flips.

In effect, Ⓛ rewards parts that came out with more material than the worst case allows. A hole drilled smaller than LMC (still legal under the size tolerance) earns position bonus equal to how much smaller it is.

Look at the diagram

A worked example for the same hole as lesson 12 — Ø8.0–8.4, this time with a position tolerance of Ø0.2 Ⓛ. The table walks the four manufactured sizes, but now departure is measured from Ø8.4 (LMC) down to MMC. At LMC the allowed position tolerance is the stated Ø0.2; at MMC (Ø8.0) the bonus is +0.4, total Ø0.6.

Compare side by side with lesson 12's MMC table: the totals are the same Ø0.2…Ø0.6 range, but the size at which each total applies is swapped. MMC gives bonus at LMC size. LMC gives bonus at MMC size.

Common mistake

Confusing which size earns the bonus with which size has more material. Under Ⓛ, the bigger position bonus shows up at MMC size (because that's the departure from LMC). That looks backwards if you're carrying over an MMC intuition — but it's correct. The modifier names the reference end of the size range, not the bonus end.

RFS: the silent default

What it controls

What happens when no material-condition modifier is shown in the tolerance block. The answer in ASME Y14.5-2018 is RFS — Regardless of Feature Size. The stated geometric tolerance applies at every produced size, with no bonus, no scaling, no discount.

RFS is the strictest of the three modes and the most expensive to manufacture, because every part has to hit the full position (or orientation, or profile) tolerance no matter how its size happened to come out. There is no symbol for RFS in the FCF — its presence is implied by the absence of Ⓜ or Ⓛ.

Look at the diagram

The same Ø8.0–8.4 hole, this time with the FCF | ⌖ | Ø0.2 | A B C | — no modifier. The tolerance-vs-size plot is a flat line at Ø0.2 across the entire size range. No wedge, no bonus, no growth. The same constraint at every hole size.

Below the plot, three FCFs sit side by side: Ⓜ (cream, wedge growing toward LMC), Ⓛ (red, wedge growing toward MMC), nothing (cream-dim, flat line). Same drawing geometry, three completely different inspection outcomes — driven entirely by which little circled letter you did or did not write.

Common mistake

Assuming that "no modifier" means "use whatever modifier is conventional in this shop." It doesn't. The 2018 standard is explicit: absence equals RFS. If you wanted Ⓜ, you had to draw Ⓜ. (Older pre-1994 drawings flipped this default — assume RFS only on Y14.5-1994 or later, and check the title-block standard before you decide.)

Straightness: line elements or derived axis

What it controls

How straight a feature actually is. Straightness is a form control — no datum needed — and it works in two distinct flavors depending on where you put it on the drawing:

- Surface straightness: arrows pointing at the surface. The tolerance applies to line elements along that surface, one slice at a time. Each line on the cylinder must lie between two parallel lines that are t apart. - Axis straightness: the FCF attached to a size dimension (with Ø in the tolerance block). The tolerance applies to the derived axis of the feature. The axis must lie inside a cylinder of diameter t.

The second form is dramatically more permissive in practice and is the one used on shafts and bores that need to mate over their length.

Look at the diagram

Top half: a slightly banana-shaped shaft. The straightness arrow lands on the surface, so the tolerance zone is two parallel lines in the longitudinal plane — applied to every line element around the cylinder. The shaft's surface may wave gently, but each individual line stays inside its 0.05 strip.

Bottom half: the same shaft, but now the FCF hangs off the Ø dimension with Ø0.05 in the tolerance block. The tolerance zone is now a cylinder of diameter 0.05 containing the derived axis. The whole shaft can bow as long as its axis stays in the tube.

Common mistake

Drawing the FCF in the "axis" location but forgetting the Ø prefix on the tolerance. Without Ø, the inspector reads it as line elements; with Ø it's a cylindrical axis zone. One little symbol decides whether you're inspecting the skin or the spine.

Flatness: two parallel planes, no datum

What it controls

How flat a planar surface is. Flatness is a self-referencing form control: the tolerance zone is two parallel planes a distance t apart, and the entire surface must lie between them. No datum is needed — flatness doesn't care which way the surface is tipped, only whether it could be sandwiched between two ideal planes that close t apart.

Surface plates, gasket faces, seating surfaces for bearings — anywhere a flat-to-flat mate carries load or seals — get flatness.

Look at the diagram

A surface that's slightly warped, drawn with its high and low points exaggerated. Above and below the surface sit two parallel reference planes (blue) at t = 0.08. Every point on the real surface lies between them. The two planes are free to float and tilt to find the tightest enclosure — the inspector isn't told where to put them, only that they must be parallel and t apart.

The FCF on the side shows the flatness symbol (▱ — a parallelogram tilted) and the bare tolerance value. No datum letter — because there isn't one to use.

Common mistake

Confusing flatness with parallelism. Parallelism is flatness with a datum — it constrains the surface to be parallel to some other reference. Flatness alone says nothing about orientation; it only says the surface itself can be sandwiched in a thin slab. If a part is wedge-shaped but each face is perfectly smooth, both faces pass flatness — even though they are wildly out of parallel.

Position: the workhorse

Position with diameter symbol

Position at MMC: the bonus chain

Position + MMC + datums at MMC = bonuses compound. This is where GD&T pays its biggest dividends. Walk through with a hole pattern.

Composite position tolerance

Stacked FCF for one feature: upper line controls the pattern relative to datums; lower line controls feature-to-feature within the pattern. Tighter inner zone.

Position vs concentricity vs symmetry

Concentricity and symmetry are deprecated in Y14.5-2018 — use position or runout instead. Old drawings still show them.

Profile of a line

Tolerance zone is two offset curves following the basic profile, in any cross-section. 2D control on a 3D surface.

Lesson 31 · §11 · Profile tolerances In the queue

Profile of a surface

Two offset surfaces following the basic profile, applied across the whole feature. The Swiss-army-knife symbol — controls form, size, orientation, location all at once.

Lesson 32 · §11 · Profile tolerances In the queue

Unilateral vs bilateral profile

Phantom lines on the drawing indicate which side of the basic profile the zone sits. Default = equal bilateral split.

§12 Runout 00 / 02
Lesson 33 · §12 · Runout In the queue

Circular runout

Surface variation in a single rotation, measured at one cross-section. Catches roundness + axis wobble in one shot.

Lesson 34 · §12 · Runout In the queue

Total runout

Like circular runout but measured along the entire length while rotating. Catches taper and straightness errors circular misses.

Projected tolerance zone

Pushes the tolerance zone outside the part — used for threaded holes where the bolt extends through and the bolt axis is what really matters.

Lesson 36 · §13 · Advanced In the queue

Composite vs multi-single-segment FCF

Composite FCF: lower line ignores rotation from secondary/tertiary datums. Multi-single-segment: all datums apply on every line. Subtle and easy to misread.

Lesson 37 · §13 · Advanced In the queue

Customized datum reference frame

Translation/rotation modifiers on a datum letter let you intentionally relax constraint at one datum while keeping it elsewhere. Newer Y14.5-2018 feature.
